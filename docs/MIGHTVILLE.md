# Mightville — the movement

**Status**: Concept capture
**Captured**: 2026-06-13
**Domain**: `mightville.com` (appeared AVAILABLE as of 2026-06-13)
**Relationship**: the *movement / ideology*. [[DEYVILLE]] is the *platform / tool* that the movement uses. Mightville is the **why**; Deyville is the **how**.

---

## 1. The thesis

A political movement for **radical decentralization**: a world of self-governing **towns and cities** rather than nation-states and their administrative subdivisions — countries, states, cantons, oblasts, Länder, régions, provinces.

The core claim about subdivisions: **they are powerless, centrally-imposed, and unstable.** They don't govern themselves — a central government draws their borders, assigns their powers, and redraws them at will. They are administrative conveniences of the center, not communities with real agency.

## 2. The argument (the case the movement makes)

- **Imposed from above, not chosen from below.** A central government decides a province's borders and powers. The people inside had no say in the lines drawn around them.
- **Unstable by design.** Subdivisions get merged, split, and renamed for political convenience. *(Concrete example: France consolidated its 22 metropolitan régions into 13 in 2016 — by central decree, over local objection. Borders that can be redrawn from the capital were never really the people's.)*
- **Powerless.** Most subdivisions can't tax, spend, legislate, or decide in any way that the center can't override. They administer; they don't govern.
- **The town is the real unit.** A town is small enough to be legible and accountable, large enough to provide for itself, and bound by actual community rather than a line on a ministry's map. It's the scale at which self-government is real.

**Conclusion:** if towns can govern themselves transparently (treasury, law, voting, procurement — see [[DEYVILLE]]) and federate voluntarily for the few things that need scale, the country and its subdivisions become obsolete middle layers.

## 4. How to express this across the projects — calibrated, not blanket

> The instruction was "reflect it in the code and marketing of all relevant projects." Do this **deliberately**, because the projects are not all the same kind of thing. Blanket-injecting an "abolish countries" stance everywhere has real downside (below).

**Tiering:**
- **Mightville (own site):** full voltage. This is the movement's home — manifesto, the argument, the call to action. Be as bold as you want.
- **Deyville (the platform):** strong movement alignment. Deyville *is* the movement's tool, so "govern your town, not your nation" is on-brand here. Lead with empowerment ("a town that governs itself") more than negation ("abolish countries").
- **OrangeCat / FleetCrown (commercial, customer-facing):** **benefit-framing, not movement-framing.** These have real users and at least one real customer. Market the *value* — decentralization, local control, transparency, freedom to participate with any identity — which is fully aligned with Mightville **without** requiring a visitor to sign onto "abolish all countries" to use the product. Link out to Mightville for those who want the philosophy.

**Why the tiering (honest risk):** "Abolish countries and subdivisions" is a polarizing political stance. Stamped across a commercial product's marketing it can alienate customers, users, partners, and payment processors who want the tool but not the politics — and it's hard to walk back once it's the brand. Keeping the movement loud on its own property and benefit-framed on the commercial products gets you the alignment without betting the businesses on a political position. The movement is the soul; the products are the proof — don't make buying the proof require buying the whole ideology.

## 4b. Virtual towns (the launch communities)

Mightville's first towns are **virtual** — founded and self-governing *on the platform* before any physical territory. This is the wedge: prove the model with real communities running real treasuries/votes (via [[DEYVILLE]]), legally and immediately, with the sovereignty story as the north star rather than a blocker.

**The day/night pair (recommended launch):** **Dayville** + **Nightville** — a clean, intuitive duality. Two towns with distinct character/ethos; a person belongs to one. This is also the simplest concrete instance of the Genesis "different people, different version" idea: same platform, two cultures, you live in one.

**Themed expansion:** **Knightville** / **Knightsville** — a chivalric/medieval-flavored town in the family.

### Naming hazard (decide before registering more domains)
The names collide hard:
- **Deyville** (the *platform*) and **Dayville** (a *town*) are near-homophones — spoken aloud, in search, and in typed domains they're indistinguishable. Running both guarantees confusion. **Pick one role for the "Dey/Day" sound**, or rename one.
- **Knightville** vs **Knightsville** differ by one letter — keep *one*, not both, unless they're deliberately distinct places (and even then, expect typo-traffic leaking between them).

Recommendation: **Dayville + Nightville** as the launch duality (strongest, most legible), keep **Deyville** strictly as the platform name, and hold **Knightville** as a later themed town — dropping **Knightsville** to avoid the one-letter collision. Register town domains only after the role/spelling is locked.
